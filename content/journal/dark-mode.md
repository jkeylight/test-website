There's a lazy version of dark mode that amounts to inverting a light design, swapping `#fff` for `#0d0d0d`, and calling it done. The result always looks wrong — heavy where it should feel expansive, murky where it should feel rich.

Dark mode done properly is a completely different design problem. The same rules don't apply. Most of them work in opposite directions.

## Why dark is harder

On a light background, contrast is your primary tool. Dark text on light paper is a pattern so deeply embedded in human visual culture that almost anything you put there will be legible. You have to work hard to fail.

On a dark background, you lose that safety net. Without the contrast affordance, typography has to work much harder. Letter-spacing needs to open up — tight tracking that looks crisp on white reads as a smear on black. Line-height needs more air. Weight choices become critical: a typeface that looks elegant at regular weight on light becomes invisible on dark.

## What dark mode is actually communicating

Dark interfaces carry cultural meaning. They signal: serious, premium, focused, nocturnal, confident. They're associated with professional tools, luxury products, and things that happen after hours. None of that is accidental.

> Dark interfaces ask more of everything — typography, hierarchy, hierarchy, contrast. There's nowhere to hide.

When Figma ships a dark UI, it's signalling: this is a tool for people who mean business. When a luxury fragrance brand builds a dark website, it's signalling: we don't need to shout. The darkness is the confidence.

This is why dark mode done badly is so obviously bad. The darkness has an implicit promise attached to it — a promise of quality, intentionality, craft. When a dark site looks cheap, the contradiction is jarring.

---

Dark mode is not a trend, and it's not a preference. For the right brand, it's the only honest visual language. And for every brand that chooses it: the bar is high, and it should be.
