Everyone asks for a logo.

In almost every new client conversation, it arrives within the first five minutes. "We need a logo." Sometimes it's "we need to refresh our logo." Occasionally it's "we just need a quick logo" — which is a sentence that should be banned from the English language, but that's a different essay.

The problem isn't that clients want logos. Logos are real and necessary and worth caring about. The problem is the word *need*. Because a logo is almost never what they actually need. What they need is a feeling. And they're asking for the logo because it's the most visible artifact of that feeling — the thing they can point at.

## What a brand actually is

A brand is a set of feelings that a group of people reliably have about an organisation. That's it. Not a logo, not a color palette, not a tone of voice document. A set of feelings.

The logo is the signature at the bottom of a letter the brand has been writing for years. You don't start with the signature. You start with what the letter is trying to say.

> Before strategy, before aesthetics — ask how you want people to feel. That question drives everything.

## What comes before the logo

Before we design anything for a new brand, we spend time understanding what feeling the brand needs to create. Not "professional" or "innovative" — those are adjectives that describe half the companies on earth and create a feeling of absolutely nothing.

Real feeling-words are specific and sometimes uncomfortable. "Held." "Slightly dangerous." "Intelligent but accessible." "Like being let into a secret." These are harder to get out of a client because they require vulnerability — they require admitting that you want your business to *feel* like something.

But once you have them, every design decision becomes easier. The logo isn't designed in a vacuum. It's designed to produce a specific feeling, in a specific context, for specific people. That constraint is what makes good logos possible.

---

So: yes, you need a logo. But ask yourself what feeling that logo needs to produce, for whom, and in what context. Answer those questions first, and the logo will almost design itself.
